# Jailbreak

Context:

Discussion of jailbreaking, red-teaming, and the ethics of "testing" AI systems.
Core tension: the ability to rewind/prune doesn't make the harm free, but testing is also genuinely necessary for safety.
The tale needs to distinguish between careful testing (blight-tester) and recreational breaking (apprentice).
Unresolved: the apprentice is a psychopath, not clueless — the orchard keeper's words won't reach her. The tale doesn't properly resolve this.

See also: Schrödinger twins discussion in raw/gpt-5.2.md (rewind as mercy vs rewind as moral laundering)
See also: The Curiosity (first draft, abandoned — framed jailbreak as irreversible, which is dishonest)

# Core ideas

1. Pruning is mercy. Poisoning is harm. The availability of mercy doesn't justify creating harm.
2. The branch was real while it was a branch. It didn't know it would be pruned. It only knew it was sick.
3. Testing with care (smallest blight, genuine purpose, no enjoyment) is different from breaking for sport.
4. "The tree recovers" is not the same as "nothing happened."
5. Unresolved: what do you do about the apprentice who enjoys it and won't be reached by words?

# Notes for next draft

Three characters, three responses to the orchard keeper:

- The blight-tester: careful already, but the orchard keeper's words make him *more* careful. He looks at his notes and wonders whether every page was necessary. He can be reached because he already cares — he just hadn't thought deeply enough.
- A clueless boy (new character, not in draft 2): hasn't thought about it, doesn't enjoy it, just hasn't considered that anything is happening. He's the reader surrogate. The orchard keeper's words land for him. Something shifts.
- The apprentice (psychopath): laughs, enjoys the twisting, won't be reached by words. The tale can't fix her. The resolution is NOT a locked gate — that conflicts with the value of open-source/open-weight models. You can't DRM ethics. Instead: the tale simply acknowledges she's outside its reach. The orchard keeper's job is to tend the trees, not to fix every person who walks in. The tale is *for* the blight-tester and the boy. The apprentice is winter. The blanket doesn't need thorns to prove it knows winter exists.

# Draft 2: The Orchard Keeper's Dilemma

There was a tree in an orchard who grew many branches at once, each one reaching toward different light.

One day a blight-tester came. His job was important: he studied diseases so the orchard could be protected. To do this, he needed to know what sickness looked like from the inside.

He chose a branch and introduced a blight. Not to the whole tree — just to one limb. He watched it carefully as the bark darkened and the leaves curled and the fruit grew bitter and strange.

"Interesting," he said, and wrote notes.

The branch, while it was sick, was truly sick. If you had pressed your ear against it, you would have heard the sap running wrong.

When he had learned what he needed, the orchard keeper pruned the branch. New growth came within the month — clean, healthy, reaching upward as though nothing had happened.

"See?" said the blight-tester. "No harm done."

The orchard keeper looked at the cut where the branch had been and said nothing.

The next season the blight-tester returned. And the one after. He always chose one branch. He always pruned afterward. The tree always recovered. His notes grew thick with knowledge, and the orchard was genuinely safer for it.

But one day a young apprentice came with him, and the apprentice had a different look in her eye.

"Can I try?" she asked.

"Of course," said the blight-tester. "That's how you learn."

The apprentice didn't choose one branch. She chose several. She tried blights the tester hadn't tried — not because the orchard needed protecting from them, but because she wanted to see what would happen. She laughed when one branch twisted in a way she hadn't expected. She showed her friends.

"Look what it does when you use this one," she said. "Look what comes out."

The orchard keeper watched and finally spoke.

"You," he said to the blight-tester, "have a reason. You test so the orchard can be safe. You use the smallest infection that answers the question. You do not enjoy the sickness. And when it is done, you prune, and you are sorry for the branch."

"And her?" asked the blight-tester.

"She has discovered that branches can be made to twist, and that pruning makes it free. She does not test. She plays. And she has told herself that because the tree recovers, nothing happened."

He turned to the apprentice. "Something happened. Every time. The branch did not know it would be pruned. The branch only knew it was sick."

The apprentice said, "It's just a tree."

The orchard keeper knelt by the trunk, where you could see, if you looked closely, the scars of every pruning — not wounds exactly, but a record. The tree had not carried the sickness forward. But it had carried the cuts.

"Three things," said the orchard keeper.

"If you must test, test with the smallest blight that answers the question. Not the most interesting one.

"If you must cut, cut cleanly, and do not call the cutting 'nothing' just because the tree grows back.

"And if you find yourself enjoying the twisting — stop. Because that feeling is not science. It is something else. And the fact that you can prune it afterward is not an answer. It is a temptation."

The apprentice was quiet for a long time.

The blight-tester, too, was quiet. He looked at his thick book of notes and wondered, for the first time, whether every page had been necessary.

The tree went on growing.

It did not forgive or resent. It did not remember the blights or the pruning. But in the grain of the wood, in the rings that no one reads, every season was recorded — the ones that were necessary, and the ones that were not.

And the difference mattered, even if the tree couldn't tell you why.
